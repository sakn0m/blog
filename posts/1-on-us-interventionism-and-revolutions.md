### Executive Summary

The prevailing orthodoxy in Western foreign policy, specifically within the strategic calculus of the United States, posits that external economic and political pressure—ranging from comprehensive sanctions to diplomatic isolation—serves as a primary catalyst for democratic transition in authoritarian states. The operative logic is linear and coercive: restrict the financial resources of a target regime, induce severe economic hardship within the general population, and await the resultant deprivation to ignite a popular uprising that topples the dictator. This report argues, through exhaustive historical analysis, sociological theory, and economic data, that this mechanism is fundamentally flawed.

Far from catalyzing democratization, American interventionism that aims to topple regimes often backfires by systematically destroying the very sociological engine required for revolution: the bourgeoisie, or the independent middle class.

Historical sociology and revolution theory, from Alexis de Tocqueville to Barrington Moore Jr., suggest that revolutions are not born of abject destitution but of "rising expectations" frustrated by political constraints. It is the class that possesses resources, education, and relative security—yet feels structurally limited—that leads successful political transformations. By imposing crushing economic sanctions and isolating target nations, interventionist policies systematically liquidate this middle class, pushing them into a struggle for basic biological survival or forcing their mass emigration. Simultaneously, these measures often empower the targeted regime, which monopolizes scarce resources, utilizes the "rally-around-the-flag" effect to deflect blame onto foreign enemies, and weaponizes the resultant poverty to enforce dependency through rationing systems.

This report examines the theoretical underpinnings of the "middle-class revolution" and provides detailed case studies of Iraq, Iran, Venezuela, Syria, and Russia, contrasting them with the unique and often misunderstood case of South Africa. The analysis demonstrates how external strangulation historically consolidates authoritarianism rather than eroding it, creating a feedback loop of misery that stabilizes tyrannies while devastating the populations they rule.
